  • I also want co op where no one falls hopelessly behind

Copy pasting items for everyone solves the second but kills the first. So drop that idea. You need a structure that keeps everyone inside the same power band while still letting builds diverge.

Here is a concrete way to do it that is not a traditional XP puddle system.

1. Use a shared run power curve

Have one shared run progression track that drives when the party gets stronger.

Examples of what can fill it:

  • Rooms cleared

  • Bosses killed

  • Core pickups (batteries, scrap, souls, whatever)

Call this team_rank or run_level.

All enemies, difficulty, and upgrade tiers use this number, not per player XP.

Effect: everyone climbs the same staircase at the same time, even if one player is doing more killing.

2. Per player builds, synchronized by tiers

Instead of duplicating items, you keep upgrade tiers in sync.

Data model idea:

text
team_rank: int          # 1, 2, 3, ...
player_rank[p]: int     # usually == team_rank or team_rank - 1

Invariant you enforce:

  • player_rank[p] >= team_rank - 1

  • player_rank[p] <= team_rank

Meaning no one is more than one tier behind the party.

When the team_rank increases:

  1. Freeze the game for a mutation draft event.

  2. Each living player gets a choice of upgrades appropriate to that tier.

  3. When they pick, player_rank[p] = team_rank for that player.

If someone died and gets revived later, you can auto grant them a couple of catch up mutations until they are at team_rank - 1 at minimum.

You just made keeping pace a system rule instead of something you fuzz by drop luck.

3. How upgrades actually look in game

Do not make all 4 players get the same upgrade. That is boring and also unbalanced across different kits.

Do it like this:

  • Every time team_rank bumps, run a draft event:

    • Pause combat or do it between waves.

    • For each player, roll 2 or 3 upgrade cards from a pool tagged for that character.

    • They pick 1.

Make the pool per tier:

text
Tier 1: basic damage, health, movement stuff
Tier 2: build defining perks
Tier 3+: weird synergies, big spikes, tradeoffs

The keys here:

  • Everyone gets something at the same times

  • The strength of the options is balanced by tier

  • The shape of the upgrades differs per character

So the shotgun guy and the railgun guy both gain about +X effective DPS at rank 2, but via different perks.

4. Handle drops without desync

If you still want random guns and artifacts on the ground, you need rules that do not trash your pacing.

Use these classes of drops:

  1. Team pickups

    • Currency, keys, map reveals, team cores

    • Either auto distribute or instantly affect all players

  2. Choice objects

    • Chests, altars, shrines

    • Spawn 2 to 4 items at once

    • Team decides who takes what

    • When an item is taken, the others vanish

  3. Role locked drops (optional)

    • Item appears ghosted except for the best matching character (only they can pick it up)

    • Very useful if you have strong class identity

And underneath that, you can run a simple rubber band:

  • Maintain power_score[p] for each player

  • When deciding who items are intended for, weight toward the lowest power_score

  • This is just a heuristic: gun rarity, number of relics, etc

You do not need to expose this at all. The game just happens to favor the weak guy a little.

5. Four starting characters that stay on the same curve

Do not overcomplicate the base kits. They just need:

  • Unique starting weapon and passive

  • Different starting stats by a small amount

  • Same overall growth curve via the shared tiers

Example pattern:

  1. Breaker

    • Short range spray weapon, more health, slower

    • Tier 1 perks: spread control, armor

    • Tier 2: lifesteal, armor explosions

  2. Ranger

    • Precise rifle, faster move, less health

    • Tier 1: reload speed, crit chance

    • Tier 2: ricochet, wall piercing

  3. Engineer

    • Weak gun, deployable turret / trap

    • Tier 1: turret damage or duration

    • Tier 2: extra turret, tether beams

  4. Occultist

    • Weird control weapon, status effects

    • Tier 1: bigger curses, DOT duration

    • Tier 2: curse spreads on kill, enemy bullets converted

Every tier event hits all four characters. The power budget of their tier 2 perk options is roughly equal, even though what they actually do is different.

6. Death and re entry without ruining pacing

If someone dies early, you do not want them coming back as a wet noodle.

Simple rules that work:

  • When a player respawns:

    • Set player_rank[p] = max(team_rank - 1, 1)

    • Auto give them catch up generic perks from earlier tiers

    • Give them a decent baseline weapon, not their starting pea shooter

If you want extra tension, you can make them respawn with:

  • Less max health for this run

  • One fewer reroll or heal opportunity

They are weaker, but not useless, and still inside the same power band.

  4. Treat all on ground loot as:

    • Shared money

    • Occasional choice chests spawning 2 guns, 2 artifacts

Only later add the fancy power scoring or role locked drops.

If you try to micromanage fairness with only drop tables, you will keep fighting edge cases. Use the shared rank to define the spine of the run and treat drops as flavor on top, not the main progression.

  • Simpler code: AnimatedSprite2D or AnimationPlayer runs one sheet, weapon sprite just follows.

Cons

  • Any big redesign of the character means editing multiple frames.

  • If you want tons of cosmetic variants, it gets expensive.

Pros of segmented (head/body/legs) approach

  • Easy to swap helmets, robes, armor colors.

  • You can reuse the same leg walk cycle for multiple characters.

Cons for your case

  • At 16x16, segment seams are very visible and ugly.

  • You still have to tune per-part offsets for each animation.

  • You lose a lot of expressive motion in robes and halos.

3. What I would actually do for your classes

For each class:

  • Body sheet:

    • 2 frame idle (slight bob or breathing).

    • 4 frame walk left, 4 frame walk right.

    • 2 or 3 frame "fire" pose that you can blend into.

  • Weapon as a separate sprite:

    • Rotated toward aim direction.

    • On attack, snap forward one or two pixels and spawn muzzle flash / shockwave.

  • Optional:

    • Halo as a separate sprite with its own tiny 2 frame pulse if you want to reuse halos across skins.

So, no arms, no segmented legs, just "body + halo + weapon" and a small number of carefully drawn frames. That will look cleaner and cost you a lot less animation time than trying to be modular at this resolution.
